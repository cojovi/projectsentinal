## TL;DR

A Ninth Circuit ruling on Tile/Life360 quietly affirmed that companies can update Terms of Service via email, and your continued app use counts as agreement—even if the email landed in spam and the underlying dispute involved stalking facilitated by consumer tracking technology. The legal logic is narrow, but the signal is broad: "notice" in the platform era may have become a compliance checkbox rather than actual communication.

---

## The Case That Arrived in the Spam Folder

Late last month, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit issued a [memorandum](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/memoranda/2026/03/03/25-403.pdf) addressing a deceptively simple question: If a company emails you updated Terms of Service, and you later open the app, have you legally agreed to those new terms?

The answer, according to the panel: Yes. Even if you never saw the email. Even if it went to your spam folder.

The plaintiff—a woman who had allegedly been stalked using Tile's consumer tracking technology—argued she couldn't have consented to arbitration because she never received meaningful notice of the updated terms. The court disagreed. Under California contract law, the email constituted adequate notice, and her subsequent use of the app constituted assent.

## The Hacker News Reaction: Contract Theater

The ruling landed on [Hacker News](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47305461) like a digital rights grenade. The comment section reveals precisely why this case resonates with tech-literate audiences who've watched "consent" become increasingly performative:

> *"Every single ToS is written to benefit the company, and when necessary, harm the consumer. They feel like the legal equivalent of Calvin Ball. So long as you just stash it in a ToS, you can apply any stupid rule your lawyers can imagine."*

The thread captured a growing recognition: we've normalized a version of contract formation that would be unrecognizable to traditional contract law. The "meeting of the minds"—that fundamental requirement that both parties actually understand and agree to the same thing—has been replaced by compliance theater where inbox placement, app reuse, and unread boilerplate reshape legal rights without meaningful human participation.

## The Mechanics of Constructive Notice

The court's reasoning hinges on a legal doctrine called "constructive notice"—the idea that certain actions or publications put people on sufficient legal alert, whether they actually saw the notice or not. It's the same principle that lets courts say you should have known about a newspaper ad even if you don't read newspapers.

But applied to modern platforms, constructive notice becomes something stranger: a legal fiction where technical compliance substitutes for actual communication. The company sent the email. The user opened the app later. Therefore—legally—notice was received.

The problem, as the plaintiff's situation illustrates, is that this mechanism breaks down in practice. Emails get filtered. Inboxes overflow. Terms of Service updates—often dozens of pages of dense legal text—are functionally unreadable for normal humans with normal attention spans.

## Stalking, Surveillance, and the Consent Paradox

What makes this case particularly sharp is the context: the dispute involved allegations that Tile's tracking technology was used for stalking. The plaintiff sought to hold the company accountable. Instead, she found herself bounced to individual arbitration based on terms she claims she never saw.

This creates an uncomfortable tension. Consumer tracking products—designed to help people find lost keys or track family members—can be weaponized for surveillance. Yet the legal mechanisms designed to hold companies accountable for harm can be neutralized by consent frameworks that require users to predict future harms and opt out of fine-print legal clauses they've never read.

## The Broader Pattern: Procedural Ambush

The Tile ruling isn't isolated. It's part of a pattern where platform-era "consent" increasingly resembles procedural ambush with a login screen:

1. **Unilateral modification**: One party can change the terms at any time
2. **Constructive notice**: Email counts as delivery, regardless of whether it was seen
3. **Implied assent**: Continued use constitutes agreement, even if the user was unaware terms changed
4. **Arbitration clauses**: Disputes get funneled to private venues, often favoring companies

Each step is arguably legal. The cumulative effect is a system designed to ensure users are bound by terms they didn't read, sent to venues they didn't choose, resolving disputes before they become public or precedent-setting.

## The Naked Truth

What the Hacker News thread gets right—and what the court opinion dances around—is that modern digital consent has become a magic trick. The sleight of hand works like this:

- **Puff of smoke**: A company updates its Terms of Service
- **Misdirection**: An email gets sent (and maybe filtered, maybe buried, maybe ignored)
- **Prestige**: Continued app use becomes "meaningful assent"

But here's the uncomfortable question the ruling forces us to ask: If a contract can be formed without either party reading it, without meaningful negotiation, without even ensuring the other party received notice—is it still a contract?

Or has "consent" become just another digital exhaust product, harvested by platforms and processed into legal defenses?

The Ninth Circuit answered the narrow question: Yes, under California law, the arbitration agreement is enforceable.

The broader question—whether this system of constructive notice and implied assent serves anyone but the platforms that designed it—remains very much open.
