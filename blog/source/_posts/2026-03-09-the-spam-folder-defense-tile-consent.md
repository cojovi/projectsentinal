Every tech company swears you consented. You just don’t remember doing it, because the magic trick happened while you were trying to live a normal life. Tile and Life360 just got the courts to applaud that trick. 

**TL;DR**: A Ninth Circuit panel said Tile’s emailed Terms of Service update plus later app use equals binding arbitration—even when the warning allegedly fell into spam during a stalking dispute. The ruling shows how “notice” and “assent” now mean “we sent something, and you opened the app eventually,” not “a human actually agreed.”

<!-- more -->

## When “notice” lives in spam

The case comes straight from a [March 3 Ninth Circuit memorandum](https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/memoranda/2026/03/03/25-403.pdf). Tile (now owned by Life360) emailed users a Terms of Service update in 2021. Buried in that update was an arbitration clause. One plaintiff later alleged Tile’s trackers were used to stalk her, so she sued. Tile waved the email and said, “Sorry, arbitration only.”

> **Reality Check**: The opinion never claims the plaintiff read the email. It just says the email was sent, and continued app use therefore equals assent under California law.

The panel leaned on what lawyers call constructive notice. Translation: if the company can show it tried, the law pretends you saw it. That’s believable when we’re talking about a certified letter or a notice nailed to your front door. It’s laughable when the “door” is your Gmail Promotions tab.

## Arbitration dressed up as consent

The dispute wasn’t about a feature tweak. It was about allegations of stalking using a consumer tracking gadget whose marketing copy has always danced on the line between safety and surveillance. Instead of exploring whether Tile had a duty to police abuse, the court short-circuited everything with arbitration.

- **Unilateral change**: Tile pushes new terms by email.
- **Minimal proof**: A log entry that the message left their server.
- **Assent by inertia**: You open the app weeks later, therefore you “agreed.”
- **Escape hatch**: Arbitration quietly removes the fight from public courtrooms.

> **Key Insight**: Arbitration clauses aren’t neutral housekeeping. They dictate who gets to see the evidence, what remedies exist, and whether anyone else ever learns the company lost.

## Hacker News smelled the contract theater

The [Hacker News thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47305461) that latched onto this case reads like a focus group of fed-up power users. People aren’t railing against arbitration because they love lawsuits; they’re railing because the process feels like Calvinball.

> **Community View**: “Every single ToS is written to benefit the company… stash it in a ToS and you can apply any stupid rule your lawyers can imagine.”

Commenters mocked the ritual of forcing users to scroll through dozens of screens and press “Accept” while everyone involved knows the contract isn’t truly being read. That cynicism matters because it shows the legal fiction is collapsing. When a concept requires universal pretend-play to function, it’s ripe for abuse.

## Constructive notice meets human reality

Constructive notice is supposed to balance efficiency with fairness. In practice, platform-era notice has drifted into four easy steps:

1. Blast a template email to millions of people.
2. Hope spam filters show mercy.
3. Assume future logins equal comprehension.
4. Argue in court that the checkbox moment was “voluntary.”

That might pass legal muster, but it fails every usability test. People have:

- **Notification fatigue**: We triage inboxes because there are 200 “updates” a week.
- **Limited time**: Reading a 5,000-word contract every quarter is fantasy.
- **Power imbalance**: Saying “no” often means losing hardware already purchased.

> **Reality Check**: The plaintiff here didn’t even deny owning the device. She denied meaningfully agreeing to a private arbitration clause slipped in after the fact.

## Procedural ambush disguised as product updates

Modern consent architecture has quietly shifted from “meeting of the minds” to “paper trail strong enough to survive summary judgment.” That architecture thrives on ambiguity:

- **Inbox placement** decides whether you keep your right to sue.
- **“Use equals assent”** converts autopay inertia into legal contracts.
- **“We mentioned it in terms”** absolves platforms from explaining change impacts in plain English.

It’s the same logic automakers use when they disable vehicle features until you accept a fresh license on the dashboard. Refuse, and you lose functionality you already purchased. Accept, and you might be signing off on mandatory data-sharing or forced arbitration. Either way, your “choice” was fabricated.

## The stalking context raises the stakes

Tile markets itself as a safety product: keep tabs on keys, pets, family members. That’s exactly why civil liberties groups flagged the brand years ago—anything that can protect can also stalk. When someone alleges harm from misuse and wants to test whether the company acted responsibly, being told to enter private arbitration feels like being locked out of your own story.

If the legal system is going to treat email blasts as meaningful notice, it should at least require:

- Evidence the message was delivered and opened (not just sent).
- Plain-language summaries up front, not paragraph 47.
- Opt-out mechanisms that don’t require clairvoyance.
- Independent review when the dispute involves safety or criminal allegations.

None of that happened here. The court simply shrugged and said, “You used the app. That’s enough.”

## What needs to change

The ruling technically follows precedent, but precedent hasn’t caught up with the dopamine-loop reality of phones, spam filters, and anti-harassment tech. We need new guardrails:

- **Legislative clarity**: Define what constitutes adequate notice for post-purchase contract changes. Spoiler: it’s not a single bulk email.
- **Consent refresh limits**: Require affirmative clicks for high-impact clauses like arbitration or data resale, even for existing users.
- **Audit trails**: Make companies prove that users actually saw the clause, not just the subject line.
- **Abuse-sensitive exceptions**: If a product is implicated in stalking or domestic violence, forced arbitration should be presumptively invalid.

> **Pro Tip**: If you ship hardware that can track people, assume your ToS will one day be Exhibit A in a stalking case. Design your consent flow like lives depend on it—because sometimes they do.

## Key takeaways

- **Legal notice ≠ human notice**: Courts are letting email logs override reality.
- **Arbitration is a pressure valve**: Companies can deflate public scrutiny before a case even starts.
- **Spam folders now decide rights**: That should terrify anyone who relies on digital tools for safety.
- **Public backlash matters**: The Hacker News response is a warning shot—a savvy audience sees the gap between “consent” on paper and consent in practice.

---

If the internet keeps pretending that scroll-through contracts equal thoughtful agreement, we’ll keep normalizing procedural ambush as a business model. Tile may have won this round, but the backlash shows people are done applauding the magic trick.
